Indian Classical Scales

Discover the foundations of Hindustani and Carnatic music traditions. The Thaat system provides the framework for Indian classical music, with each scale expressing unique emotional and spiritual dimensions.

Understanding the Thaat System

The Thaat (also spelled That) is a framework of seven primary scales in Hindustani classical music. Each Thaat represents a unique tonal structure and serves as the foundation for multiple Ragas, which are specific melodic frameworks used in performance.

Key Features: Indian classical scales are based on a 22-note octave (compared to Western 12-note), allowing for microtonal variations called "srutis" that create the distinctive character of Indian music.

🥁Bhairav

The regal and solemn Thaat, characterized by its serious and devotional mood. Often used for morning performances.

Sa Re Ma Pa Dha
Time: Early Morning
Mood: Serious, Devotional
Famous Raga: Bhairav, Ahir Bhairav

✨Kalyan

The auspicious and bright Thaat with a raised fourth note, creating an energetic and celebratory character.

Sa Re Ga Ma# Pa Dha Ni
Time: Evening
Mood: Joyful, Auspicious
Famous Raga: Yaman, Kamod

💫Khamaj

Known for its light and relaxed nature. Often used in folk-inspired classical music and popular ragas.

Sa Re Ga Ma Pa Dha Ni♭
Time: Afternoon/Evening
Mood: Light, Relaxed
Famous Raga: Khammaj, Sarang

🎼Bilaval

The natural major scale Thaat, which corresponds to the Western major scale. Pure and natural sounding.

Sa Re Ga Ma Pa Dha Ni
Time: Midday
Mood: Pure, Natural
Famous Raga: Bilaval, Bhoop

🌙Marwa

A rare and mystical Thaat with unusual intervals, creating an ethereal and spiritual atmosphere.

Sa Re♭ Ga Ma# Pa Dha♭ Ni
Time: Night
Mood: Mystical, Spiritual
Famous Raga: Marwa, Bhairav Marwa

🎵Purvi

An elegant Thaat often associated with romantic and introspective moods. Frequently used in evening ragas.

Sa Re♭ Ga Ma# Pa Dha♭ Ni
Time: Evening
Mood: Romantic, Introspective
Famous Raga: Puriya, Adana

About Hindustani Scales

Hindustani classical music has evolved over centuries in North India. The Thaat system provides a systematic way to understand and practice Indian classical music. Each Thaat is like a "parent scale" from which multiple Ragas can be derived. A Raga is a specific melodic framework that defines rules about which notes to use, how to approach them, and in what order they should appear.

Master musicians often spend decades learning and performing a single Raga, exploring its infinite variations and emotional depths. The flute, particularly the bamboo Bansuri, is considered one of the most expressive instruments for Indian classical music.
